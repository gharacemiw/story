I was running from something. I was racing along the final stretch of my path (the crown of a tree). Beside me ran an enormous worm—or caterpillar.

"Well, this is where I die," I said to the caterpillar. I had reached the top of the tree, and there was nowhere left to go.

"Climb on me," she said.

I laughed. I didn't believe she was going to help me—perhaps because she was the one chasing me. But seeing no other option, I climbed up. And the worm-caterpillar flew away, with me perched on top of her head.

Then she left me in a place that wasn't a place at all, but a choice.

"Choose what you want to be next," the caterpillar told me.

There were six or seven options. I remember: *butterfly* (though you had to begin as a caterpillar), a *plastic stone* hollow in the middle, stamped with manufacturing marks and carrying a smell like the plant pots we bought for the house last week. There was also *seashell* (white or red) with the sound of the ocean inside, and *fly*; and other options I can't recall.

I wandered from one side to the other, perhaps leaning toward the butterfly. Even in the dream, I knew those things were ideas: that the butterfly carried exuberance within it, while the seashell held a simple, intimate beauty. And still, I woke without choosing.

Between sleep and waking, I thought about my firefly story, and I kept repeating: *I am the firefly, I am the firefly, and I carry the light through my stories, and I am not beautiful in the daytime like the rest of them.*

But there was no firefly among the options the caterpillar gave me. Perhaps there will be, when I finally write that story.

*G., November 8, 2023, Wednesday morning. Today I work at Nike from 1 p.m. to 9 p.m.*

And so I emerge, miraculously whole, from yet another dive into the Zonetérea.
