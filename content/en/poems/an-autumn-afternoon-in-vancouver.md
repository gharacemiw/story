---
title: "An Autumn Afternoon in Vancouver (2022)"
date: "2022-09-17"

The smell of coffee,
the yellow light of cafés escaping then breathing
into September's cloudy air.
The quiet streets, now and then sliced by
the minimalist lines of Teslas and Polestars,
Ubers in a hurry,
fat, bearded men
always smoking or chewing something
inside trucks that aren't theirs,
family SUVs with a single occupant
because the kids are at school.

It all happened on a Wednesday afternoon
when the sidewalks carried
skinny girls in Lululemon sweats,
talking into their
white white white earbuds,
walking their dogs,
who smelled of damp grass,
then questioned the air and
didn't bark,
Middle Eastern men,
loud when young,
quiet when old,
cyclists near Science World,
couples sitting on wooden benches,
looking out at the bay,
men in suits eating at restaurants on Burrard,
talking about trivialities
of utmost importance.

Ten kinds of ham in downtown grocers,
thirty-seven types of cheese,
waiting to be chosen, cut, shredded.
Laptops open at Starbucks,
delivery guys on e-scooters and e-bikes
tapping the crosswalk buttons on the poles,
waiting their turn,
rolling past
little apartments, full of small succulents
or maybe ferns,
the shelves, the desks, the dark Mac screens,
chairs with bamboo legs,
and the cabinets and the rugs and
the Peruvian cotton throws draped over the sofas,
all visible from outside, in a cozy nakedness,
those apartments in their silences,
resting from their owners.

And the walls with graffiti drawings,
breweries that will open in an hour,
men working construction,
their hard hats covered with stickers
that say things like *rock 'n' roll*, and *EastVan.*
the crazies on the streets, the food carts:
hot dogs, shawarma, ice cream, tacos,
at the station, an old man strummed a guitar while blowing
the melody of *Wind of Change* on a pan flute,
and in the open guitar case in front of him
two fivers and some loonies,
I thought:
I hope he hasn't been there for many hours.

The dry maple leaves on the ground,
and maybe it was from them the smell came,
a soft, unforgettable smell
that vanishes every time I look for its source,
and that makes me think:
*Vancouver is speaking to me*
and I wonder when I'll be able to
see the light in your sparkle,
laugh at your jokes,
drink your saliva at a restaurant in Yaletown,
buy you in a shop on Robson Street,
park a car between your shoulder blades,
sit in a café and
write without feeling like an idiot.
See funny shapes in your clouds,
wander through your secrets,
take from you *everything* and
much more than you took from me—
for it was a lot—
and so I'll be able to belittle you
and yet thank you.

That afternoon what I did was
remember home,
the feeling of heavy rain cutting warm air,
my parents, my friends,
the way my dog would flop on his back,
the taste of a *Louvada Pilsner*
the smell of my old car,
the sound of Brazilians laughing,
the dull little lagoon beside my house and all the capybaras.

I wasn't after nostalgia,
I only wanted to push death away.
To stretch out my spirit
as I can stretch out my hand
and then touch
the soul of Vancouver.

—Gustavo, somewhere in 2022
