I'd promised myself I wouldn't work in a kitchen again. The pact happened in a coffee bar, after one of the worst shifts at the juice shop. It was nine PM and snowing outside. I was stinky, sweating under my winter jacket, sipping a cup of coffee. Coltrane's sax floated around me. I swore I'd beg, that I'd hawk watercolors on dirty sidewalks, that I'd draw the darkest obscenities. I would live or die through what came out of the tip of a pen.

And yet, there I was again...

It had probably been the most respectable building on Bute Street a century ago. Now it was surrounded by glass high-rises, a grandfather ringed by grandchildren who'd grown up and gotten much taller than him. Still, it kept its charm: the entrance was neoclassical, with marble details on the cornices and balustrades, window latches of polished copper, all that jazz.

I was greeted by the little Japanese woman who'd interviewed me over the phone. She was fond of me, I think. She led me through a series of labyrinthine parquet hallways. As we walked, she showed me around:

"This is the laundry. Our members wear custom-tailored pieces, so all our staff is specialized."

We also passed a hallway running parallel to East-Asian-style massage studios, two banquet halls, a ballroom, a bar-restaurant… The little corridors spread through the Club like capillaries, hidden under the skin. There were rooms tucked away in the middle of nowhere, crammed with coffee machines, refrigerators of all ages, silverware, emergency kits.

My interview was in one of those rooms. Across the table sat the woman and the chef, a big redheaded man with a gleaming smile. He held my resume in his hands.

"Pleasure to meet you, I'm Meles. I see here you worked at Tideview House. That's a good kitchen, but you only stayed a few months."

"It is indeed a wonderful kitchen, sir. It was my first experience in the hospitality industry. I stayed long enough to learn what mattered, but I don't tolerate being disrespected."

The little Japanese woman leaned over the table.

"Oh, *disrespected*? What happened?"

It wasn't a difficult interview. After a year in Canada, I'd become a minor-league job sharpshooter. The turnover in menial jobs is huge, and managers and chefs don't have the time or inclination to look too deeply into your case. You need to give them a certain portrait, a simple character. I was the Latino in his mid-twenties who'd dropped out of law school in Brazil to start over in Canada. Orderly, moldable.

"We pay eighteen dollars an hour," the Chef told me at the end of the interview, "and here, there are no tips."

I scratched my chin and gave him my noblest look.

"The starting salary doesn't matter to me at all. I'm here because I was told there's room to grow in this company. I want to start, show what I can do, and we can talk about money again in a year."

They hired me on the spot.

* * *

Adil had feminine eyes, long lashes, and a square jaw dotted with stubborn stubble. He'd been born in a country that no longer existed, since it had been invaded and absorbed by China.

The other dishwasher was a refugee from Ukraine. When they were together, Adil and this guy were so contrasting they looked like cartoon characters; Adil had hair black as oil, the Ukrainian was blond. Adil was old, the Ukrainian was young. Adil was short, the Ukrainian was tall. Adil was always in a bad mood, the Ukrainian had a semi-permanent smile on his face. Maybe the only thing they had in common was the tragic fate of their countries. They received me and trained me restaurant-style: I was thrown into the fire like a piece of bacon.

"Banquet today!" Adil told me. "Busy night, *very* busy!"

"Banquet today!" the blond echoed, rubbing his hands with excitement. I never understood his passion for frantic shifts. He simply loved the torture.

"I can wash very fast! Look!" he'd say. And then he'd grab seven plates at once, bits of tomato and meat sauce dripping between his fingers. He'd line everything up on the tray, then slide it, *hurl* the tray into the machine. Then he'd do it again and again like he was on some great mission. He was always smiling. He had thin lips curved in an M-shape, looked like Quentin Tarantino's mouth.

The dishwashing machine was huge. The dishwashers' station was practically a hallway parallel to the kitchen, and the machine ran from one wall to the other like a great snake. Conveyor belts carried the dishes through its stainless steel throat, and from the sound of the jets inside, it was impossible they wouldn't come out clean on the other side.

My first shift started at three and went until midnight. Around eight, a man in a suit called all the waiters over and started organizing them into a line.

"What's happening, Adil?" I asked.

"Go see for yourself."

They lined up right in the middle of the kitchen, between the prep stations. The main course was already plated; it was a thick piece of filet with sauce and vegetables. The air above the meat shimmered with heat. The plates went on trays, silver cloches on top so the food wouldn't get cold.

"I want everything uncovered at EXACTLY the same time?" said the head waiter. "Always from the right, with the right hand. When I say bon appétit, on the PÉ in apPÉtit you remove the lid. Do you all understand?"

"YES!" the waiters answered. They wore white linen shirts with their last names stitched on the corner, napkins draped over their forearms. I wondered what happened to left-handed waiters.

There was a TV on the wall, showing what was happening in the banquet hall. Men and women in fine clothes were sipping wine and waiting for their food. The security camera made their eyes white, like in nighttime animal footage.

Adil called me back.

"Pretty fancy," I said.

"Fifteen thousand dollars a year, just to be a member."

"Damn! Who knows, maybe one day it'll be us, huh?"

He looked at me, maybe for the first time.

"You'd have to be pretty stupid to spend fifteen thousand dollars on something like that."

"What would you do with the money?"

"Buy a car. A 2015 Corolla. Now start putting the glasses in the machine. When they're done stuffing their faces, we'll have more work."

The blond suddenly turned from the sink and stared at me with his very blue eyes.

"I bet I can fill this tray in under a minute, WATCH!"

And his performance began again…

* * *

Over the weeks, I discovered that being a dishwasher at the Club wasn't just standing by the machine. There was a daily ritual. I'd check with Adil, then go downstairs to collect the staff lunch dishes. The food wasn't as good as what the members got, but it was good. When there were leftovers, we'd pack ourselves a generous container before throwing the rest away.

"A crime!" Adil would say, as he helped me dump pounds of rice into the trash. "But those are the rules…"

"Why don't they donate it to some charity?"

"Ah, *donate*, you say. And if someone gets sick? Goes to the hospital? It would be our fault! They'd call all the cooks in front of the judge."

Sometimes there were leftovers from the members' banquets too, and then we'd get to take fine dishes home. I took home leftovers that were probably worth more than my wages for the whole shift.

"One of the best perks here," Ricardo told me.

Ricardo was Brazilian too. He had two jobs at the Club: waiter and maintenance man—that's what you do when you have three kids in Vancouver. He pointed at the trash can under the sink.

"Always, *always* put two bags in these thirty-five-liter bins. Do you have any idea how PISSED I get when we're throwing the bags in the dumpster and one of them bursts at the bottom? Man, if I catch the asshole who…"

He had a beer belly and that capacity to switch between relaxation and focus that only a man working two jobs develops. Besides that, nothing tied him to any piece of land: he'd lived in England before COVID, and before that, in some other country. He was the type of man who could very well get rich on his own, or go through a war without going completely insane.

"Where are you from, back in Brazil?" he asked.

"Cuiabá."

"Your girlfriend too?"

"Yes."

"I have an uncle there! He's a city councilman, Elil Soares."

"I think my father's mentioned him once. Maybe they even know each other!"

We jotted down names and other details about our families to check if they actually knew each other. The subject never came up again.

Every now and then, the blond Ukrainian would summon me to help him. The Club had hired me because they wanted to promote him to cook soon, so he was determined to make a real dishwasher out of me. Since I was extremely slow to learn, I didn't complain.

"Let's clean the skeleton," he told me one day.

We stopped beside what looked like a stainless steel cement mixer. A delicious smell of meat escaped from it.

"The meat's gone," he said. "It was cooked for ten hours and served at the last banquet. Still, *We, the Cooks*, use the water that's left to season risotto and soup. I just drained it."

There was a spigot on the side of the structure for the juice to come out. Underneath were two steaming buckets of brown water. What actually remained inside the mixer were pieces of bone. The sides were marked with dried fat, splashes of seasoning, tomato skins. We had to pull out what we could with our hands, then scrubbed the rest with soap and sponge. It was a slow process, requiring several trips to the sink.

I was dumping out one of the dirty buckets when I saw Adil talking to a waitress. Her hair was pulled back in a loose bun, and I could see a crystal-shaped tattoo at the base of her neck. She had thick, defined, wonderful legs.

"Wash some more of those ice buckets for us as soon as you can, pretty please?"

"I'll try, but don't count on it," Adil answered. "We're very busy today."

"Thanks, Adil. You're *so sweet*!"

She grabbed a bucket from the middle shelf and headed back to the bar.

"She seems special, Adil," I said.

"Charlotte? She's like all the others. She always comes here looking for those buckets. Never enough. Wants everything on her time."

When I walked away, I saw him hustling to put more buckets in the machine…

* * *

Another one of my duties was, twice a day, to drag a cart through other parts of the Club stocking clean dishes and collecting dirty ones. I'd go to the pastry kitchen, where a Colombian guy and a Korean girl worked listening to pop music at full volume.

"Bring back the mixer bowl as soon as you can," she'd tell me.

"Yes, ma'am."

I'd continue my route, and they'd continue singing "you can stand under my umbrella, eh eh eh."

For certain events, the kitchen used special items kept in the Provision Room. The Provision Room was a hotel version of Smaug's cave, a tall warehouse where accessories and knickknacks had accumulated since the Club's founding. Copper and silver cutlery, candlesticks, chafing dishes with lions and roses in relief, thousands of bottle openers, cheese domes, gravy boats, Chinese porcelain tea sets, butter crocks, ramekins, bonbonnieres…

I'd walk between the shelves, sometimes picking something up to look at it closely. I'd try to memorize the design for a drawing or just imagine the people using it: the spoons entering a thousand mouths, the knives cutting a thousand fish. Generally, I'd arrive at the realization that it took a large number of rich people to sustain all of this: the laundry, the kitchens, the silver industry, and employees like me, observing silverware at four twenty-seven on a Tuesday. Well, maybe I wasn't that expensive, but I'd think: *my God, there aren't few rich people in this world, it can't be that hard to become one.*

I grabbed a set of ramekins and headed back to the kitchen. Adil was at the edge of the wall, peeking at the prep stations.

"Everything's calm. Come with me!"

He grabbed a cart, filled it with clean French presses, and we left. I got lost quickly in the hallways. We went deeper and deeper into the Club's stomach. Finally, we entered a cramped little room with a wonderful smell of tea and coffee.

"Give me two of those presses, and put the rest on that shelf," he said.

He opened one of the mini-fridges, grabbed milk and filled the carafe of a press.

"We're close to the Lounge. Keep an eye out!"

I cracked the door open and looked down the hallway. Nothing. All I could hear was the music from the massage room.

"All clear."

He steamed the milk. Filled the other press with hot water. He hopped up and grabbed an aluminum tin from one of the cabinets. The inside of the lid said: *Darjeeling Black Tea — Grown on the slopes of the Himalayas*.

Adil fished out a handful and tossed it into the press with water. He hid it on the bottom shelf of the cart and we made our way back to the kitchen. There, the Ukrainian joined us.

"Chai time?"

"Chai time!" Adil confirmed.

I watched him place three cups on the counter. He tossed a pinch of salt into each one.

"This is how we do it, in Xinjiang," he said, ceremonially.

"It was his land," the blond whispered to me.

Adil poured the black tea over the salt and swirled the cups a bit.

"You know, my son is very good at that game, League of Legends," he said.

Apparently, we were entering some kind of break.

"Really? That's great, Adil. I tried playing, but it was too hard."

When the tea settled, he added the milk. The color came out exactly the same in all three cups. It was the color of pearls in the shade on a sunny day.

"My son, he works on computer, teaching others to play. I go into the room to bring him chai, and he's there, teaching. He doesn't need to do hard work like us. It's all on the computer. He even teaches girls."

"Pretty girls!" the blond explained to me. "He must be really good!"

"Of course! They even called from the United States. Sixteen years old, he was. They wanted him to go there and play! But I didn't let him. I said: careful, son! It's government things. I thought they wanted to know something about Xinjiang."

Adil handed us the cups.

"Do you think they did?" I asked. I tried the tea. It was sublime.

"I don't know. This business with countries, very complicated. Did you know Hitler escaped to Brazil?"

"Yeah, I've heard that theory."

"He escaped! They said he died, but nobody saw the body. He was in Brazil."

"In Brazil they don't really believe that."

"See? This business with countries, complicated, many secrets. That's why I told him: son, Canada is a good country. We're going to stay here."

"And how did he react?"

"He got angry. Then sad, not talking to me."

Just then, Charlotte came looking for more ice buckets.

"Did you guys hear? There's going to be a fight at the Club! Chef Meles is going to fight!"

"What kind of fight?" the blond asked.

"Boxing. It's an event for the Provincial Boxing Association. I really want Chef Meles to win!"

She left with the buckets. Adil stared at his cup. He held it with a delicacy very rare in dishwashers.

"I got very scared," he said. "It was his dream. I should have let him go."

"It's okay, Adil."

For the next fifteen minutes we leaned against the counter, drinking our tea. We watched the cooks dumping filthy pots, sticky ladles, blackened frying pans into the sink basins.

* * *

My shift ended two hours after the main kitchen closed. I'd then head to another kitchen, where the dishwashing station was a mere corner, and a good part of my work was cleaning plates with shrimp sauce, whatever the cooks dirtied, and many whiskey glasses. I'd stare at the bottom of the glasses, trying to find fragments of conversations important men had had.

I tried not to feel anxious as the end of the shift approached, but it was hard. Closing a kitchen is grueling; there's a certain anticipation, with the cooks giving the dishwasher everything that probably won't be needed in the next hour. And when the dishes are done, you have to clean the machine itself—like a doctor cleaning a patient's intestines. The dishwasher does this while watching the cooks frantically scrub the floor, drench every corner of the stainless steel surfaces with soap and disinfectant. I'd finally take out the trash, change clothes, and walk out into my delicious freedom.

My shift ended at one AM. Last train: one sixteen. I'd clock out and run through the cold downtown streets to the station. I'd ride home watching Brandon Sanderson's writing classes, observing people coming back from their parties. On the worst days, I was on LinkedIn looking for jobs at animation studios.

Often, I'd stop at a pizza place before going home. Two Indian girls worked there. They served me, the neighborhood crazies, and the late-night crowd with a blasé look framed by black eyeliner, always over trap music in Hindi.

*Bring your child and get a balloon*, one sign said.

"ONE SLICE OF CHEESE AND ONE OF PEPPERONI," I'd shout over the counter to be heard, "AND A COKE!"

Then I'd go to the table, sit down, and just stay there.

With the right dose of exhaustion, and bombarded by verses whose meaning was unknown to you, you could start to believe you were in a mystical capsule where time never passed. You could stay there for weeks, think about EVERYTHING, and then open the door and get on with your life (if you wanted to; no rush).

But after swallowing the first slice my eyes regained focus, and I knew time was passing. I saw the fine drizzle falling under the yellow light of the streetlamps…

* * *

"Do you think we'll get to see the fight?" Charlotte asked, on the fateful day.

"I don't know. We have to work," Adil said.

"I thought you'd be there, serving people," I told her.

"I will be. What I meant is I hope you guys get to see something."

"Oh…"

When she left, Ricardo came over.

"There was a dishwasher who used to put the buckets on the top shelf sometimes, just to watch Charlie reach for them. Looks like a cat stretching, it's the most beautiful thing."

"And they don't do it anymore?"

"Nobody knows why the guy got fired, so I guess nobody risks it."

I remembered that line from Shakespeare: *for courage mounteth with occasion…*

The Ukrainian appeared.

"Meles will fight at ten!" he said, and went back to his station. He was being trained as a cook that day.

Adil and I kept washing the banquet dishes. And the cooks' dishes. Downstairs, the pastry chefs had started preparing desserts and sugary garnishes for drinks. We washed their dishes too. The staff worked with a proud determination. The Chef was nowhere to be seen. The rumors were that he was warming up somewhere in the Club. I could imagine him preparing himself for the great confrontation, throwing punches in the air in front of a large Victorian mirror…

An hour before the fight, Adil and I had to stock plates in the plate room. It was a white room, with white plates, and white platters, and white bowls. Thousands of plates lining up.

"Anybody running a little bet?" I asked Adil.

"Nobody's going to bet against Meles. What friend would bet against a friend?"

"Well, you're right."

I tried to put plates in their proper stacks, but they were always a few inches too big or too small. I ended up just looking around, stupidly searching for the right stack while Adil did all the work.

When we got back, I asked Ricardo.

"Hey, want to throw in a bet?"

"I'll bet twenty bucks Meles loses."

"Shit, me too."

"So we're even. Today I'll be at the bar. When the fight starts, I'll forget to close the curtain and you guys take a peek."

At ten, we headed there. At the end of a hallway, flanked by stacks and stacks of napkins, was the curtain. Looking to the other side was like looking through a portal.

You see, the light in a kitchen is a light designed to show the world's filth. It's a light friendly to what's raw, textured, and clinical, and enemy to human beings. The light on the other side of the curtain, however, was made for people's spirits, to feed their fantasies…

There was a spotlight on the ring, which made the mat shine and gave it a magnanimous air, like water falling at the end of a waterfall. Around it, scattered throughout the great hall, the Club members drank champagne. The light caressed their hair, defined their silhouettes, painted cozy blocks of shadow.

"Excuse me!" said a waiter, pushing through between us and going to take dirty glasses to the kitchen. We regrouped as soon as he passed.

"Ladies and gentlemen!" announced a commentator. "Here's what we've got for you tonight! In the blue corner…"

While he spoke, I watched Charlotte and Ricardo circulating, trying to distribute the appetizers and fill the glasses so they'd have time to watch. They were finally done when the bell for the first round rang.

Meles and the other guy started a shallow fight. They grappled timidly. There wasn't the killer instinct or the cold patience you see in professional fights.

"MELES!" Adil shouted beside me.

They reminded me of two kids fighting. Part of me wanted the chance to fight too, maybe I'd have some shot. But this was me: always thinking I had some shot. Meles's opponent was doing better. After one or two attempts, he managed to break through the Chef's defense and hit his ribs square on.

"MELES! MELES!" Adil shouted. He turned to me and the Ukrainian kid. "You! Cheer! Cheer! We need to cheer for him!"

The blond joined in once or twice. Some people turned around and noticed the brood of employees behind the curtain. I think we were a cartoonish group, something that could have come out of a Leyendecker painting.

After a few punches, Meles ran away like a baby who doesn't want his diaper changed. Then he managed to land a cross. The other guy returned with a limp punch. They ran from each other a bit more. The footwork was nonexistent. We tried to mentally calculate who was accumulating more points.

It was in the last round that Adil stepped out from behind the curtain.

Marching into the hall, he raised his thin, muscular arms. His eyes shone. The velvety light embraced him. The food stains disappeared from his apron, which went from dingy white to purple. He could suddenly be taken for a holy man, a member of some eccentric musical group, a ghost.

"MELES, MELES, MELES!"

People looked. Some were embarrassed, others laughed. I heard Charlotte apologizing to an elegant man. Droplets of saliva flew from Adil's mouth. Sometimes he lost his breath, his voice faltering, his chest trembling. But he'd breathe deep and resume, with even more force:

"MELES, MELES, MELES, MELES!"
