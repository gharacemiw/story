Rodrigo showed up wearing a size M T-shirt, even though he was clearly an XL, maybe even XXL. A sweat stain bloomed beneath his armpits.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey," Jefferson said. He lowered the binoculars and shook Rodrigo's hand. Rodrigo's grip was limp; Jefferson's was firm. There was sweat under Jefferson's arms too.

There was sweat everywhere on both of them. The air around them shimmered with heat. The ground was ocher-colored, dotted with dying patches of green: dry grass, stubborn clumps, scrawny trees. It was August in the Cerrado, and the sun had decided to take a closer look at the earth, lashing everything with its spirit. Down below, sunlight reflected painfully off the bodies of passing cars.

"Too damn hot," Rodrigo said.

"Hot as fuck."

Rodrigo sat down next to Jefferson. They were sitting on a cut tree trunk. The bark was thick, rough, and itchy against the ass.

"How long's it been since we last saw each other, huh?" Rodrigo said. "Since high school. A decade!"

"I miss school sometimes. We could punch each other, hook up, and play soccer all day. Just dumb shit."

Jefferson was thin, his cheekbones stretching the skin tight, beard unshaven.

"Yeah," Rodrigo said, without much enthusiasm. He didn't have very good memories of school. In fact, he didn't have very good memories of anything that had happened after he turned seven.

They talked about where the guys they'd gone to school with had ended up. They talked about the big players, their wild spirits tamed by marriage. About the pretty girls who hadn't aged well, the teachers (*Meireles still teaches there!*), the rich kids who went broke, the ones who disappeared, the ones who moved to another province. The overall feeling was that they'd already eaten the steak, and all that was left on the plate now was a wilted cabbage salad.

"I thought you'd made it in life, working with computers and all," Jefferson said.

"I just taught computer classes to some old people."

"And they died?"

"They died."

"Fuck, that's the problem with old people. Nobody left you anything in their will?"

"One of them left me a printer. I sold it for a hundred reais."

*Must've spent it on food*, Jefferson thought, but he only said:

"Better than nothing."

Jefferson went back to looking through the binoculars.

"And you?" Rodrigo asked.

"I was doing fine. An uncle of mine got me a job at SENAC after school. I worked there for six years. Pretty chill."

"That's great."

"Yeah. But then I got involved with a coworker. My wife found out. Camila—you remember her from school? She showed up there and made a huge scene."

"Oh, man..."

"I remember it like it was yesterday. It was a Friday. I thought I was about to leave, grab a cold beer. She showed up and started screaming at the top of her lungs, telling everyone I was cheating on her with the girl. Threw my boss's computer out the window, thinking it was mine. Threw the *Top 10 Companies in Mato Grosso* award out the window too. Smashed all the printers. Security managed to hold her back, but the screaming went on until the cops showed up. Half an hour."

"Jesus Christ."

"They fired me the following week. My uncle wasn't a city councilman anymore, so I had to move on to other gigs. Did odd jobs at a motel, construction, supermarket. Total bullshit. Each one of those jobs takes something from you. Construction takes your back. Retail takes your dignity."

"So what are you doing now?"

"Stealing."

"*What?*"

"Stealing. I figured out the best setup, my old friend. Back when I worked at the hotel, I met this guy who stole things. Only sometimes, when a really, really good opportunity came up. He looks like a complete idiot, you wouldn't believe it. And then, a few months later, my brother-in-law went on a trip. The guy makes good money, he's a dealership manager."

Jefferson bent down, grabbed a sweaty plastic bottle, half water, half ice, and took a few long swigs. Rodrigo watched. Jefferson went on:

"I remember him bragging to everyone about how many Toros they sold. You know that car, Toro?"

"Yeah."

"They were selling like crazy, and my brother-in-law thought he was the king of the universe. He traveled, and I tipped off my hotel buddy. He went in and cleaned the place out. My cut alone was fifteen grand. *Fifteen fucking thousand!* Now I work only like that, instead of killing myself for fifteen hundred. I don't have the guts to jump fences and break in, but I've always been... what do you call it? Observant. Even back in school, I knew a lot just by watching. Remember that?"

"I do."

Rodrigo looked more closely in the direction of the binoculars and saw there was a gated community over there. The houses were cream-white, box-shaped.

"Look, Jefferson, I respect your thing, but this kind of stuff isn't for me..."

"Oh, cut that out, man. I didn't call you here to get you involved. I was just... telling you. I don't know. Fuck."

"I know."

"And why the hell do you wear such tight shirts?"

Jefferson finally lowered the binoculars and scribbled something in a little notebook he pulled from his pocket.

"I was just explaining so you'd understand why we're in the middle of nowhere. They built that development like two months ago. They haven't even finished all the outer walls yet and an idiot over there already built a huge-ass house. There's the avenue out front, but everything else is just scrub."

"Okay. But I'm not interested. And about the shirts, I—"

"I called you because I found something that's tech-related. And you're the man, ever since school."

"I see."

"I remember you had this little gadget and could turn off the cafeteria TV whenever you wanted."

"It was just a phone app."

"You're a genius. Come with me."

Jefferson got up and pushed into the brush behind them. Rodrigo followed, his body obeying before his head had time to decide. They walked for about fifteen minutes, deeper and deeper into that flawed patch of Cerrado. Rodrigo considered the possibility that Jefferson might kill him for his organs, or kidnap him. Why the fuck hadn't that asshole just sent a photo of whatever it was on WhatsApp?

"Look, man, I've got an appointment at three," he said.

"You don't have shit."

"I'm serious."

"We're almost there."

The circle of sweat under Rodrigo's arms finally took over the entire T-shirt. The sound of cars on the avenue faded into the distance. Burrs clung to the cuffs of both their pants. There wasn't even a bird singing.

Rodrigo was about to give up when they arrived. He looked around, half-expecting a film crew.

"No way."

"Isn't that state-of-the-art or something?"

"I've never seen anything like this before, Jefferson! Are you fucking with me?"

"How have you never seen this?"

It was on the ground, half buried, half exposed. Purple. Elliptical, like a giant egg made of glass. Tiny points of starlight sparkled among cloudy veins, like the images you see in space documentaries. Rodrigo stepped closer and saw his own ugly face reflected on the glassy surface.

"Could it be amethyst?"

"What?"

"Amethyst. That stone they use in jewelry. Maybe it's even sapphire."

"Maybe it is! Holy shit!"

"But that's not possible. This thing is polished. And—"

The starry veins were moving. Slowly, but they were moving. Rodrigo looked up and saw a sequence of symbols that looked like hieroglyphs.

"What's that up there?"

"That's why I called you. Isn't that computer code?"

"No."

"Look closer! If you don't figure it out, you don't get your cut."

Jefferson was on the phone with a buddy of his, trying to rent a tow truck—without the driver. Rodrigo reached out and touched one of the symbols. The mark of his finger lit up, and then part of the object simply detached and floated outward.

"God damn! Jefferson!"

Jefferson hung up, and they went inside. The interior was much larger than the exterior. Perfectly transparent—you could see the brush all around them.

"At least it's nice and cool in here," Jefferson said. He tried to sit on the floor, but a chair rose up from it and supported his bony ass.

"My God, it's a spaceship!" Rodrigo said.

"I always knew they existed."

Rodrigo walked toward the control panel. There was nothing written on it, just a bunch of concentric circles, and—

"There's a gearshift here," he said, and moved it.

The piece of the ship that had been floating outside came back and snapped into place. They couldn't open it anymore. They were trapped.

Suddenly they saw the gated community from above, then the city, the state, the country, until finally they were staring at the round, hazy circle that is Earth.

Down below, a small transparent creature with purple eyes nudged another one and said,

"Isn't that our ship?"

---

Getting water was easy, but it was only on the fourth day that Jefferson and Rodrigo figured out the sequence of buttons that produced food. In silence, they ate bread and pot roast while the ship lazily surfed between stars and suns and moons. They saw tiny planets of every color, and on some of them there was even life.

"On that one they've started installing internet cables," Rodrigo commented.

"That one over there, they only care about partying," Jefferson said about another.

The first kiss came a year later. And you know what's surprising? It was Rodrigo who made the first move, as they passed in front of a supernova. The initial awkwardness quickly gave way to intimacy. There was a happy period, but nothing lasts forever.

Jefferson killed Rodrigo on a curve between two moons. Afterward, he crouched against the wall and cried for a long time, staring at the still body of the person he had loved the most, after his mother. The universe was silent. The universe was a piece of music.

As his tears dried, the ship began to slow down and descend onto a tangerine-colored planet. A crowd of tiny creatures was waving enthusiastically from below.
