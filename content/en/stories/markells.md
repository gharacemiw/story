By that point, there were few things in the world I wanted more than to get out of Markells. The joy of landing my first job in Canada had evaporated—fast as cheap perfume. Before I knew it, I was looking around and seeing everything: the bars, the blood, the whips.

I'd been there for about three months. The previous manager was decent enough, a chubby guy with thick Coke-bottle glasses named Anthony. He always seemed to be heading somewhere, and IN A HURRY. But he had to leave because his mother was sick. I imagined he didn't want to be selling acrylic paint while his mother was leaving this world.

The new manager was a witch. I don't remember her name, but I do remember that she was young, Canadian, and had a habit of smiling right before obliterating someone. She took over in the fall—I remember restocking cinnamon- and pumpkin-scented candles around that time.

She demanded that I start at six, even though I lived far away. That meant waking up at four, forcing something down, and watching the sun rise from the subway, surrounded by Indian women in Tim Hortons uniforms, construction workers, and Filipino ladies with rough hands. The doctors, architects, designers, dentists, office people—they were all still asleep. I felt like we were Vancouver's labor force, and we could barely wait to escape that life.

Still, once you got to the store, it wasn't so bad. It was closed to the public. We had to go down to the stockroom and drag carts loaded with cardboard boxes to the proper sections. Each employee carried a box cutter in their pocket, so they could open the boxes easily. We checked exactly where each product was supposed to go, then stocked the shelves.

There was the fine arts section, with paints, sketchbooks, and canvases. But there were also yarns and needles, jewelry-making supplies, baking tools, kids' crafts, wood paint, body paint, sprays, gift packaging, seasonal items, picture frames, photo frames, iron-on prints for T-shirts, signage...

I liked the calm of opening those boxes and placing cupcake molds next to other cupcake molds, or restocking brushes, or whatever else it was. It felt like there was some order in the world. When your cart was empty, you went back down to the stockroom and grabbed another one, all without saying a word. Once a week, we had to help unload the truck that arrived very early at the warehouse.

The problem started at nine. That's when Anthony—or the new manager, later on—would walk up to the front doors and unlock them. What followed was a swarm of human beings: teenagers with sagging pants hunting for supplies for psychedelic paintings; old ladies looking for yarn and needles; mothers with hyperactive children; fathers and their daughters with pink braces buying school supplies; middle-aged bureaucrats searching for meaning in life through art; otakus looking for markers for their *Jujutsu Kaisen* characters.

It was the first time I caught myself questioning things. Was it worth it? I mean—how many tons of oil? The chemical engineering behind making a tube of gouache so this woman could paint a begonia. The machines, the excavators stabbing the earth in search of graphite. All the trees cut down, my God, all the mitochondria killed so that we could have ONE PENCIL—and all of it ending in a grotesque portrait of a cat named Charlie, or a generic drawing of a hand. How many millions of kilowatts? The logistics of transportation. The horror, the horror!

I used to think human creativity should never be questioned, but faced with all that, I couldn't help it. Maybe I was going crazy. And before you judge me: I included my own art in all of it.

As employees, we had to wear a red vest, and on the back it said: CAN I HELP YOU? That alone was enough to destroy us.

"Grab one of these," Anthony told me on my first day. He pointed to a table full of walkie-talkies in the staff room. "Then plug in an earpiece and stay alert. If a customer asks you something you don't know, just ask on the walkie-talkie and then EVERY OTHER EMPLOYEE CAN HELP YOU!"

"Alright," I said, in the English of someone who'd been in the country for two weeks.

And with the store open, we kept stocking. Always ready to provide excellent customer service. Like this:

"Hi! Hey, you. Hi!" the customer says.

"Hi... can I help you?"

"I'm looking for *saiv*j..."

"Sorry? Could you repeat that?"

"I'm looking for *tzalnxz*..."

"*Sa-tzajin?*"

"No, *tskamx*..."

"I see. Could you describe the item a bit better?"

"Hmm, it's kind of a *Belendeux* shape."

I looked around, searching for a coworker. All there was was an undefined sea of customers.

"Well," I said, "I'm not sure where that is, but I'll ask on the radio and see if someone knows."

The customer checks their watch.

"Sure."

I press the radio button.

"Hi. This is Hervé. Does anyone know if we have... saixnx?"

The customer looks up at me as if I have some kind of dementia.

On the radio, someone answers:

"What was that?"

"Can you tell me the name again?" I ask the customer.

"ZSAINKX!" the customer says.

I repeat the word on the radio, at the very peak of my abilities.

"I have no idea what you're talking about," some coworker says in my ear.

After that, silence.

"Well, we don't have it," I tell the customer.

"Okay."

I went back to stocking.

In the worst cases, that customer would eventually find the item on their own and come back to me, eyes shining with irritation and a thirst for revenge. But otherwise, the whole thing would just repeat itself:

"Hi! Hey, you. Hi! Can you help me?"

I did this for another two or three hours, trying to pretend I was fine, and then took my half-hour break. I ate a granola bar, drank some water, and read a bulletin board with customer compliments and a photo of Employee of the Month. Part of the board's permanent collection was a motivational poster:

YOU WORK WELL\
=\
WE SELL MORE\
=\
MORE HOURS OF WORK AVAILABLE TO YOU

Then I went back out and had to say on the radio: "Hervé, back on the floor."

One time, after helping a customer, the manager happened to be nearby and came over to talk to me.

"Look, Harvé. You did very well with that customer. Congratulations! But I'm afraid you're not using the correct greeting. We no longer say *Can I help you?* It's too common and tacky. Instead, we say *What can I help you with today?*"

"Alright."

"Now sign here to confirm that you understand."

One day they called me in and said I was going to be trained as a cashier. They put me next to a girl much sharper than me named Jorah. The manager said to her:

"And don't forget to explain to him how important our loyalty program is."

Jorah explained it to me.

Basically, there was a goal to convert fifty percent of non-registered customers into MARKELLS REWARDS members. Once signed up, the customer got a discount on that first purchase and was added to the promotional email list. If the conversion target wasn't met, a board of directors would call in the store manager and tear them apart for hours.

"That's because there's a board above the regional board," she said, "and if the regional board isn't doing a good job with at least fifty percent of the stores..."

In short, everyone dumped on the person below them, and we were at the very bottom. Each cashier's conversion rate was logged under their employee code. If you didn't hit the target, the manager would come talk to you. By that point, I didn't care about anything anymore.

I learned how to use the payment system, process returns, answer phone calls, request item picking for online orders, scan barcodes, and bag merchandise. All of it with a WELCOME SMILE and a DO YOU KNOW ABOUT OUR REWARDS PROGRAM? for every single customer.

I'd clock out already late for class, which ended at nine at night.

"The problem is people," I told my girlfriend one night. "The problem is people—and maybe the language. I'm going somewhere I don't have to deal with customers. A restaurant, maybe... Things will be better."
