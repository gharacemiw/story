Apart from a few loudmouths, everyone agreed she'd made it. She woke up that Wednesday with a faint headache, told the alarm to shut the hell up, and unlocked her phone.

She checked WhatsApp, the studio group chat, then opened Twitter to see what was going on. They were still talking about AIs. That gave her a small, private satisfaction. It mattered, and the art community wasn't going to let it go easily. Also, one artist had started a thread about whitewashing versus blackwashing and Black character representation in animation. The rest of her timeline was drawings, cats, meet the artist posts, and a tweet from Obama. The headache lingered.

She showered and then meditated for ten minutes. She made scrambled eggs and ate them with toast and thin avocado slices, sprinkled with salt and black pepper. She drew the curtains open, turned on the computer, opened her Gmail, and set herself to available on Slack.

The nine o'clock meeting was smooth. Positive feedback from the art director; only two of the props needed adjustments. She was buzzing for the premiere of the next season, for how it would shine on her portfolio. Her family (especially her little sister) always posted a photo of her name in the Netflix credits—it was a whole event.

Her apartment was small, but cozy. Light wood floors, little plants, two cats (Milo and Caju), posters of her favorite artists and friends on the walls. In one of them, Ponyo ran across an exuberant wave made of fish.

She answered an email from the T-shirt shop, following up on print negotiations. Replied to a few Instagram DMs, opened a question box in her stories to answer during lunch. And then she opened Photoshop.

That was when Carolina Duarte, twenty-nine, known as *@carol_dwarfss* across every social platform—the same Carolina who, in two months, would have been invited to move to California as Lead Background Artist at a small studio, if things had gone the way they normally did—realized she couldn't draw.

She tried to sketch a face, but it came out as a ball with two disconnected dots. She laughed, then tried again and again. Finally, she tried a cube. She watched, horrified, as childish lines spiraled into chaos between lopsided squares. "They were supposed to be more... I don't know." The term she was reaching for was *vanishing point*, but there was nothing like that in her mind.

Carolina set the Cintiq pen aside and went to get some water. Came back and stared at the scribbles she'd made. A pop-up appeared on the screen:

> Your Adobe subscription expires in three days. Don't forget to renew at *www.adobe.com/creativecloud*.

She closed it. She opened Discord and was about to call Caio when her phone rang. Unknown number.

"Hello?"

"How are you, Miss Duarte?" said a woman's voice.

"Who is this?"

"I was with you during the night."

"What?"

"You can't draw, can you? If you hang up, you'll never know why."

Carolina started shaking.

"What the fuck is this?"

"I'm part of a very special group. We stole your technical knowledge overnight. Clean job, I must say. We accidentally picked up your *crepioca* recipe, but apparently that's it. Wait—" there was the sound of typing "—looks like we've got your memories of *Pride & Prejudice*, the Keira Knightley one. So you can watch it again like it's the first time."

Carol started laughing.

"There's a lunatic for everything. Have a nice day, sweetheart—"

"This number won't be available again. This is your only chance to talk. I'm just warning you."

Carol tried, desperately, to draw Darella—the character she'd created for a webcomic that never launched. A redheaded version of herself, with little bunny ears. What came out could've been done by her mom, or anyone, really.

"Shit!" She was crying.

"So it goes," the other woman said.

"How did you do this?"

"Biotech. That's all I can tell you. Now there's a programming language for the brain. It's expected to become legal in Taiwan in four or five years—until then, you're not going to see anything about it."

"Okay. I'm hanging up now."

"Do this: go to the bathroom and look in the mirror. Right behind your right ear there'll be a mark from our work."

She ran to the bathroom. The mark looked like a fish-eye. It hurt when she touched it. She went back to her phone.

"You can't do this to people's dreams!" she sobbed. It was starting to turn into anger, and the woman on the other end could feel it.

"Listen, Carol. Don't you ever want to start over? Don't you ever want to crawl out of that velvet-and-paint-and-pixels hole you've been burrowing into since you were a teenager?"

"My life is mine! I LOVE this! You fucking bitch!" It had been years since Carol had cursed at another woman like that.

"The world is a big place," the other continued, "and I'm here to prove it. You can treat this theft like a curse, or you can step outside for the first time in your life and see other gears turning. Your skills will be up for auction on the deep web in two weeks. I'll email you instructions. I guarantee they won't go for more than a hundred thousand dollars. We stole from a lot of people, and what you've got isn't that special. Think about it. You're a knife with no tip, no edge—think about it."

"Listen here—"

"Decide, one more time, who you are. You're not fifteen anymore."

And she hung up.

Carol finally started a call with Caio. Caio, her friend, a fellow artist. He was doing a commission for some guy on Reddit. The D&D character was a thieving elf, big forearms, runes written across his forehead. After hearing the story, Caio was in shock.

"File a police report, girl!" was all he said.

Noon was creeping in. She looked out the window, for the first time free of any urge to transpose what she saw. A truck was going around the roundabout, and it wasn't a conjunction of shapes anymore. An elderly woman walked by with a grocery bag, with no sign of being a character; no gesture, no composition. The wind gently swayed the wires on the utility poles. It was *so unfair*.

She spent half an hour watching it all. Her stomach growled and she ordered a burger on DoorDash, pudding for dessert.

She waited stretched out on the couch, hands together on her belly, staring at the ceiling.
